Understanding Convolutions on Graphs

Oct 04, 2022 in Paper Notes

Introduction

Neural networks traditionally used to operate on fixed-size or regular-structured inputs are unable to elegantly process graph-structured data. Graph neural networks (GNN) are a family of neural networks that can operate natually on graph-structured data.

The challenges of computation on graph

lack of consistent structure: the example of predicting whether a given molecule is toxic.

node-order equivariance: our algorithm should not depend on the ordering of the nodes of the graph.

scalability: graphs can be really large.

Problem setting and notaion

problems on graph

  • node classification
  • graph classification
  • node clustering: grouping together similar nodes based on connectivity
  • link prediction: predicting the missing links
  • influence maximization: identify the influential nodes

A common precursor in solving many of these problems is node representation learning: learning to map individual nodes to fixed-size real-valued vectors (called representations or embeddings).

Generally, GNNs compute node representations in an iterative process. Each iteration can be thought of as the equivalent of a 'layer' in standard neural networks.

notation

  • G, V, E
  • : the individual feature for node v
  • : the representation of node v after the k^{th} iteration
  • M: a graph property

Extending convolutions to Graphs

Ordinary convolutions over grids are not node-order invariant, because they depend on the absolute positions of pixels. And the structure of node neighborhood differs from node to node.

This convolution can be thought of two steps

  • aggregating over immediate neighbour features
  • combing with the node's own feature

By iteratively repeating the 1-hop localized convolutions K times (i.e., repeatedly passing message), the receptive field of the convolution effectively includes all nodes upto K hops away.
